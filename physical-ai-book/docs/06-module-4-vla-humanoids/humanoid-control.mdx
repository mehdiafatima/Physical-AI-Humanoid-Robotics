## 6.1. The Grand Challenge of Humanoid Control

Humanoid robots inherently possess a high number of degrees of freedom (DoF) and operate on a small, dynamic base of support, making stability a continuous and paramount challenge.

## 6.2. Balance and Stability: The Zero Moment Point (ZMP) <FaBalanceScale />

The **Zero Moment Point (ZMP)** is a cornerstone concept in bipedal locomotion. It is the point on the ground where the total moment generated by gravity and inertial forces is zero. For a robot to remain stable, its ZMP must stay within its support polygon (the area defined by the robot's feet in contact with the ground).

### Control Strategies for ZMP:
-   **Preview Control**: Predict the future ZMP trajectory based on a desired motion plan and proactively adjust joint torques to maintain stability.
-   **Model Predictive Control (MPC)**: A more advanced technique that optimizes a sequence of control inputs over a finite time horizon to satisfy ZMP constraints while achieving a goal.

## 6.3. Locomotion Techniques: Walking and Beyond <FaRunning />

Achieving robust bipedal locomotion involves various techniques, from slow, deliberate steps to dynamic, human-like gaits.

### Common Locomotion Approaches:
-   **Static Walking**: The ZMP is always kept well within the support polygon. This method is slow but highly stable and easier to implement.
-   **Dynamic Walking**: The ZMP is allowed to move towards the edges of the support polygon, enabling faster and more natural-looking motion by leveraging the robot's momentum.
-   **Pattern Generators (CPGs)**: Rhythmic joint trajectories, often inspired by biological central pattern generators, are used to create cyclic movements like walking and running.
-   **Reinforcement Learning (RL)**: An increasingly popular approach where AI agents are trained in simulation to discover complex and adaptive walking gaits, even over uneven terrain.

## 6.4. Whole-Body Control and Manipulation

Humanoids must often perform tasks that involve their entire body, such as reaching, grasping, and pushing, while simultaneously maintaining balance. **Whole-Body Control (WBC)** is an advanced control methodology that coordinates all of the robot's joints and contact forces to achieve multiple objectives simultaneously while adhering to physical constraints.

### WBC Objectives:
-   **Balance Maintenance**: The highest priority task, typically using ZMP or Center of Mass (CoM) control.
-   **Task Execution**: Achieving desired end-effector poses for manipulation.
-   **Posture Control**: Maintaining a desired overall body configuration.
-   **Constraint Satisfaction**: Adhering to joint limits, avoiding self-collision, and respecting environmental contacts.

:::info Whole-Body Control
WBC often formulates the control problem as a prioritized, constrained optimization problem, solving for the optimal joint torques or accelerations at each time step.
:::
